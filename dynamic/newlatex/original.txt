                        Abstract. Forecasting scientific topic popularity from historical biblio-
                        metric signals is important for hotspot monitoring, strategic research
                        planning, and evidence-informed science policy. We formulate this prob-
                        lem as multivariate annual publication-volume forecasting across corre-
                        lated topics or subfields, where the main challenges are coupled cross-
                        topic dynamics and incompletely observed relations. To address these
                        issues, we propose AG-FC, an adaptive graph forecasting framework
                        built on the Fully Connected Gated Graph Architecture (FC-GAGA)
                        backbone. AG-FC replaces the original graph gate with adaptive topol-
                        ogy learning and further introduces SVD-initialized adaptive or hybrid
                        modes to combine latent dependencies with co-occurrence priors. Exper-
                        iments on OpenAlexsubsets (4 domains and 4 representative computer-
                        science-related subfields) show that AG-FC is strongly competitive over-
                        all (12 top-1 cells out of 24), with substantial gains at subfield level
                        (11/12 metric cells) and competitive domain-level performance. Relative
                        to FC-GAGA and its graph-gate-removed variant, AG-FC wins 20/24
                        and 21/24 cells, respectively. While adaptive-only can maximize raw nu-
                        merical accuracy, the SVD-enhanced hybrid mode preserves structural
                        priors for interpretable scientometric analysis while remaining highly
                        competitive in forecasting errors.

                    1  Introduction
                    Forecasting how scientific topics evolve over time is a core problem in sciento-
                    metrics, information science, and research management. Topic-level trajectories
                    reflect changes in disciplinary structure, research paradigms, and technology
                    fronts, and are therefore useful for hotspot identification and policy-oriented
                    planning.
                      In this paper, we define topic trend popularity as publication volume over
                    time and study a multivariate forecasting problem: given historical annual pub-
                    lication series of multiple related topics or subfields, predict their future publi-
                    cation counts jointly. This problem is practically important for both researchers
                2  Anonymous Author(s)
                and policy makers, because reliable publication-volume forecasts can support
                emerging-topic discovery, strategic planning, and resource allocation.
                 Compared with univariate trend extrapolation, this multivariate setting in-
                troduces three core challenges. (C1) Coupled temporal dynamics: topic tra-
                jectories are not independent, and strong cross-topic interactions violate the i.i.d.
                assumptionbehindmanyclassicalforecastingpipelines.(C2) Incomplete rela-
                tion observation: predefined co-occurrence graphs capture only explicit links
                and may miss latent, time-varying dependencies among topics. (C3) Prior-
                graph utilization uncertainty: in this task, it is nontrivial to determine
                whetherandwhenbase-graphpriorsshouldbecombinedwithadaptivelylearned
                relations, rather than using adaptive topology alone.
                 Given these challenges, an appropriate backbone should be robust for low-
                frequency yearly series, able to separate long-term trend from short-term per-
                turbations, and stable under limited effective sample size. In publication-volume
                data, yearly trajectories typically mix smooth disciplinary drift with bursty fluc-
                tuations triggered by new methods, events, or policy shifts. FC-GAGA is a prag-
                matic starting point because it inherits the N-BEATS backcast/forecast residual
                decomposition [18,17]: iterative residual correction helps separate low-frequency
                trend components from higher-frequency residual variation without relying on
                heavy recurrence. Evidence from long-span macroeconomic forecasting also sug-
                gests that N-BEATS-style residual subtraction remains effective when available
                temporal points are limited, where heavier Transformer-based alternatives are
                not consistently superior [27]. We therefore adopt FC-GAGA as a temporal back-
                bone for C1, rather than as a claim of unique optimality. Its original graph gate,
                however, is relatively naive and mainly prior-dependent, which is insufÏcient
                for C2–C3 where latent relation discovery and prior-adaptive fusion are both
                required.
                 Building on this rationale, we propose an Adaptive Graph Fully Connected
                framework (AG-FC). In AG-FC, residual fully connected blocks model coupled
                multivariate temporal patterns, a Graph WaveNet-style adaptive graph gate
                learns latent cross-topic relations [32], and an SVD-guided fusion design provides
                a controllable mechanism to use (or skip) base-graph priors when learning adap-
                tive topology. AG-FC also supports a random-initialized adaptive-only branch
                as a degenerate setting.
                 Our core contributions are as follows:
                1. We reformulate topic trend popularity forecasting as a multivari-
                  ate time-series problem with topology-aware modeling. Instead of
                  predicting each series independently, we jointly forecast annual publication
                  volumes across topics or subfields and explicitly model inter-series depen-
                  dencies using co-occurrence graph structure.
                2. We introduce adaptive graph learning to improve prediction and
                  uncover latent relations. By replacing the naive FC-GAGA graph gate
                  with a Graph WaveNet-style adaptive adjacency module, AG-FC learns po-
                  tential cross-topic dependencies that are not directly observed in predefined
                  graphs, while improving forecasting accuracy.

                                2    Related Work
                                2.1    Research Topic Trend Prediction: Problem Definition and Task
                                       Scope
                                Forecasting scientific topic evolution from historical bibliometric signals is in-
                                creasingly used to support emerging-direction discovery, strategic research plan-
                                ning, and evidence-informed science policy. Prior work has therefore studied
                                trend prediction with multiple observable signals, such as topic frequency, publi-
                                cation volume, citation-derived impact, and composite popularity indicators [33,
                                3,24,20,10].
                                    Because these signals encode different semantics, the literature under the
                                same label of “topic trend prediction” is methodologically heterogeneous. At the
                                proxy level, publication-based and frequency-based signals are often used for
                                timely trend tracking, while citation-based signals are commonly used to char-
                                acterize impact with a longer response lag [3,33,20,10]. At the task level, related
                                studies can be grouped into four lines: (1) regression-style forecasting of future
                                popularity values [33,3]; (2) emerging-topic identification and ranking [10,24,
                            4      Anonymous Author(s)
                            20]; (3) relation-aware forecasting using neighboring-topic interaction signals [4,
                            36]; and (4) descriptive trend analysis focused on historical shifts. This taxon-
                            omyclarifies that similarly named studies may optimize fundamentally different
                            objectives.
                               Ourstudyispositioned in the forecasting line and formulated as multivariate
                            time-series prediction. We jointly model annual publication-volume trajectories
                            across multiple topics or subfields, instead of treating the problem as emerging-
                            topic classification or ranking. This formulation follows prior evidence that topic
                            trajectories are coupled and that independent univariate extrapolation may miss
                            cross-topic effects [36,33,4].
                               The remaining distinction is how inter-topic relations are represented. Exist-
                            ing pipelines typically start from observable relation priors (e.g., co-occurrence
                            links), which encode explicit and relatively stable associations but may omit
                            weak, newly forming, or directionally asymmetric dependencies that are still
                            predictive. For this reason, recent adaptive graph forecasting studies emphasize
                            data-driven topology learning to complement predefined structure and recover
                            hidden dependencies [32,2]. Based on this scope, Section 2.2 reviews method
                            evolution in topic trend prediction, Section 2.3 discusses multivariate time-series
                            foundations, and Section 2.4 summarizes adaptive graph methods.
                            2.2   Methodological Evolution in Research Topic Trend Prediction
                            Following Section 2.1, this subsection reviews methodological evolution in the
                            forecasting line, with emphasis on the reasons each generation of methods be-
                            comes insufÏcient for topic systems with sparse, evolving relations.
                               Early studies mainly relied on statistical extrapolation such as ARIMA [34,
                            1]. These models are effective for stable univariate trajectories, but their linear
                            assumptions and weak treatment of cross-series interaction limit performance
                            whentopic dynamics are nonlinear and mutually coupled. Deep sequence models
                            (e.g., RNN, LSTM, and Transformer families) improve nonlinear fitting capacity
                            [6,7,23], yet they still operate on regular Euclidean tensors and do not explicitly
                            model graph-structured topic dependence.
                               To bridge this gap, several studies inject relation signals through feature en-
                            gineering or sequence-level coupling, such as neighborhood features built from
                            node2vec embeddings combined with DTW, as well as correlated cross-venue
                            sequence modeling [4,3]. These strategies can improve forecasting, but relation
                            modeling remains indirect: topology is precomputed outside the forecasting ob-
                            jective, and higher-order or weak links are easily lost when relations are flattened
                            into auxiliary features.
                               GNN-basedforecasting addresses this issue more directly by integrating tem-
                            poralmodelingandgraphpropagation[31,36,33].However,existingtopic-focused
                            GNN pipelines still depend heavily on predefined static graphs and relatively
                            vanilla message-passing designs, so their quality is bounded by prior-graph com-
                            pleteness. In scholarly data, co-occurrence priors are often noisy, partial, and
                            time-varying. This leaves a clear gap: forecasting models should not only use
                                                       AG-FC for Multivariate Topic Popularity Forecasting       5
                                observed relations, but also learn latent dependencies adaptively. This gap mo-
                                tivates the methodological focus of Sections 2.3 and 2.4.
                                2.3    Multivariate Time Series
                                Our task can be viewed as multivariate time-series (MTS) forecasting, where
                                topic trajectories are predicted jointly rather than independently. Recent MTS
                                advances have substantially improved long-horizon modeling through sparse
                                attention, decomposition, patch tokenization, cross-dimension interaction, and
                                multiscale mixing mechanisms [37,30,14,35,11,12,29,26,25,28].
                                    Thecommonmechanismbehindthesemodelsistensor-leveltemporal-variable
                                mixing on regular grids. This mechanism is effective for capturing long-range
                                sequential dependencies, but relation modeling is mostly implicit: variable inter-
                                actions are learned as dense latent mixing patterns, without explicit distinction
                                between observed topology and unobserved dependencies. In scientific topic sys-
                                tems, where relations are sparse, asymmetric, and time-varying, purely tensor-
                                based coupling can underutilize structured prior information while also missing
                                weak but predictive latent links.
                                    This limitation is the key methodological implication for our setting. We need
                                a forecasting architecture that keeps strong temporal modeling from MTS back-
                                bones, but augments it with explicit topology pathways and adaptive relation
                                discovery. This requirement motivates our graph-temporal design and directly
                                leads to the adaptive graph methods discussed in Section 2.4.
                                2.4    Adaptive Graph Methods
                                Adaptive graph learning has become a central direction in spatiotemporal fore-
                                casting because fixed adjacency matrices often miss latent or time-varying de-
                                pendencies. Graph WaveNet learns a self-adaptive dependency matrix jointly
                                withtemporalconvolutions, reducing reliance on purely predefined topology [32].
                                AGCRNfurtherintroducesnode-adaptiveparameterlearninganddata-adaptive
                                graph generation in a recurrent framework, enabling node-specific dynamics and
                                automatically inferred inter-node relations [2]. Compared with these methods,
                                FC-GAGAadoptsamuchmorevanilla graph mechanism (hard graph gate with
                                fully connected forecasting), so it is often treated as a naive but efÏcient baseline
                                rather than a strong adaptive-graph solution [17].
                                    Subsequent work emphasizes dynamic topology rather than static adaptive
                                adjacency. DGCRN generates time-varying graph filters and dynamic graphs
                                throughhyper-networkstyleparametergeneration,aimingtocapturefine-grained
                                temporal shifts in spatial dependency [8]. D2STGNN decouples trafÏc signals
                                into diffusion and inherent components and models them with decoupled dy-
                                namic spatial-temporal mechanisms [21]. DynAGS further studies dynamic lo-
                                calisation, where sparse communication patterns and graph structures evolve
                                over time for more efÏcient distributed deployment [5].
                                    Another line focuses on richer graph composition and scalability. STFGNN
                                introduces spatial-temporal fusion graphs to jointly encode heterogeneous local
                            6      Anonymous Author(s)
                            and global dependencies [9]. PGCN constructs progressive adjacency matrices
                            that keep adapting with incoming data during both training and inference [22].
                            Morerecently, MAGE proposes linear adaptive graph learning with a sparse bal-
                            anced mixture-of-experts strategy, targeting improved scalability and robustness
                            in graph construction [13].
                               These studies collectively show that adaptive graph design strongly affects
                            forecasting quality, and also indicate that FC-GAGA’s naive graph component
                            can be replaced by stronger alternatives. Following this evidence, we choose
                            Graph WaveNet as the replacement module and graft it onto the FC-GAGA
                            forecasting backbone. In contrast to directly porting trafÏc models, our setting
                            requires combining domain priors and latent relations for scientific topic evo-
                            lution; therefore we further introduce SVD-initialized hybrid graph fusion for
                            task-specific adaptation.

                7 Conclusion
                This paper formulates scientific topic trend popularity forecasting as a graph-
                aware multivariate time-series problem and proposes AG-FC to jointly model
                temporal dynamics and cross-topic dependencies. The model combines a residual
                fully connected forecasting backbone with adaptive topology learning and SVD-
                guided graph fusion.
                 From a theoretical perspective, our results support three points. First, latent
                relational structure is a first-order signal for topic forecasting rather than a pe-
                ripheral feature, especially when prediction targets are tightly coupled. Second,
                separating temporal decomposition and topology learning in a modular way is
                effective: a strong temporal backbone can be retained while graph design is up-
                graded independently. Third, prior-guided hybrid topology and unconstrained
                adaptive-only topology correspond to different modeling assumptions, indicat-
                ing that accuracy and relation consistency should be treated as a controllable
                trade-off rather than a single objective.
                 From a practical perspective, two implications are clear. At coarse domain
                level, gains from relation modeling are limited; at finer subfield level, relation-
                aware modeling produces substantially larger improvements. In deployment,
                adaptive only is preferable when predictive fit is the sole target, whereas
                hybrid is preferable when users also require prior-consistent relation evidence
                for expert analysis and decision support.
                 This study also has limitations. First, experiments use annual series, so short-
                cycle dynamics cannot be observed. Second, prior topology is constructed only
                from co-occurrence, which provides a single and incomplete view of real topic
                relations. Third, current relation analysis is association-based and does not es-
                tablish causal attribution. Future work will therefore focus on dynamic time-
                varying topology, multi-source priors (e.g., citation and semantic signals), and
                explicit attribution/uncertainty protocols for more reliable policy-facing use.
